Abercrombie PDP Subcategories

User Testing Round 1

Project Goals

Observe users' behavior as they interact with 5 design variations of subcategories on PDP, collecting qualitative feedback and identifying any points of friction and survey users on understandability, ease of use, perceived values, and overall preference of the design variations.

Methodology

Remote, unmoderated, task-based user study through high-fidelity prototype + questionaire

    Tested for:

  • Understandability
  • Ease of use
  • Perceived value
  • Preference

Participants

24 current/potential A&F/Hco shoppers

All participants were recruited via IntelliZoom, the participant community of our user research platform, UserZoom.

Demographics

  • 12 female, 12 male
  • US only
  • Shops for clothes online at least seasonally
  • Age 18-29
  • High school student, college-bound, or full-time student or recent graduate at a 4 year university
  • Shops within the A&F and/or HCo competitive set

Test variations - Men's

Test variations - Women's

Results

Understandability

Seeing all swatches at once best helped users understand what options are available between subcategories.

"It shows all the available variations in a single screen. We can easily compare between the 2 different styles and even between all different color options amoung those styles." - Men's test

"You can see all available options before clicking on anything, which is helpful if you aren't familiar with materials and if you're looking for a particular color." - Women's test

Ease of use

Close-cropped images helped users understand the variance between subcategories.

"It makes it clear that the exploded icon is actually bigger and the checkmark to say which one I'm on is obvious." - Men's test

"I love the close-up of the materials. I think it's very helpful to see materials up close and I think the organization of the materials and colors is appropriate and nice" - Womens's test

Perceived value

Women's test participants felt the UI with subcat images looked the most upscale, while men's participants voted in favor of tiles.

"[Tiles] have a clean look...intuitive and sleek." - Men's test

Preference

Overall, the images and swatched designs were the favorites among both men's and women's shoppers.

-However, 5/12 men's test participants selected swatched as their least favorite design.

User Testing Round 2

Project Goals

Test users on two designs and four use cases of PDP subcategories to understand if/how usability and preference changes in scenarios of more/less subcats & KICs.

Methodology

Remote, unmoderated, task-based user study + questionaire

    Tested for:

  • Understandability
  • Ease of use
  • Perceived value
  • Preference

Participants

15 current/potential A&F/Hco shoppers

All participants were recruited via IntelliZoom, the participant community of our user research platform, UserZoom.

Demographics

  • 6 female, 9 male
  • US only
  • Shops for clothes online at least seasonally
  • Age 18-29
  • High school student, college-bound, or full-time student or recent graduate at a 4 year university
  • Shops within the A&F and/or HCo competitive set

Test variations - Tiles

Test variations - Swatches

Results

Understandability

As users progressed through increasingly complex subcats/KIC scenarios, understandability of available options shifted from nearly equal on use case 1 to a 4:1 ratio in favor of tiles by use case 4.

"It seems more efficient in my decision making process to pick a style and then a color, instead of looking at everything at once."

Ease of use

User A on use case 1:

"[Swatches] allow me to see all of my design, pattern, or color options immediately instead of having to navigate between the two designs to see all of my options. A lot more captivating and less navigation to see available products all at once for this particular style of clothing."

User A on use case 4:

"I liked the same layout for the first three but it was too much on the fourth."

Perceived value

User perception of quality and value of the page design held relatively constant across use cases, except in use case 4 where users perceived tiles as the more elevated design

Preference

User preference steadily shifted towards tiles as they progressed through the four use cases.

"I think that showing all the different styles and colors works when there aren't too many options available makes a page easy to navigate. But I prefer a more compact page, especially when there are lots of different styles and options so it's not too overwhelming."

"I think the first layout [tiles] worked best in all four scenarios. I think it looked cleaner and was easiest to use no matter how many style and color options there were."
